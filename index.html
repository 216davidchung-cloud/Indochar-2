The Basics

A charcoal made by
nature's oldest process

Biochar is produced when organic materials — wood, crop residues, manure, or plant matter — are heated to high temperatures in a low-oxygen environment. This process, called pyrolysis, prevents combustion and converts carbon into a stable, porous solid.

Unlike burning, which releases all stored carbon back into the atmosphere as CO₂, pyrolysis locks carbon into a crystalline structure that resists decomposition for hundreds to thousands of years.

Indigenous peoples in the Amazon Basin practiced early versions of biochar production over 2,500 years ago, creating the legendary Terra Preta ("dark earth") soils — among the most fertile on the planet.

Why It Matters

Benefits spanning soil, water, and sky

🌱

Soil Fertility

Biochar's porous structure harbours beneficial microbes and retains nutrients that would otherwise leach away. Studies show crop yield improvements of 10–40% in degraded soils.

💧

Water Retention

Sandy soils amended with biochar hold significantly more moisture, reducing irrigation needs by up to 30% and improving drought resilience for smallholder farmers.

♻️

Carbon Sequestration

Converting biomass to biochar and burying it transfers atmospheric CO₂ into a stable form. Each tonne of biochar sequesters approximately 2.2–3.7 tonnes of CO₂ equivalent.

🧪

Reduced Emissions

Biochar application to soils significantly reduces N₂O emissions (a greenhouse gas 265× more potent than CO₂) by up to 50–80% through modified soil chemistry.

🔬

Contaminant Adsorption

High surface area and charge make biochar effective at binding heavy metals, pesticides, and other contaminants in soil and water filtration systems.

🌡️

Reduced Methane

Rice paddies amended with biochar emit substantially less methane, offering climate benefits in one of agriculture's most emissions-intensive crops.

Production

From biomass
to black gold

The biochar production process is elegant in its simplicity — ancient technology now refined with modern efficiency.

Step 01

Feedstock Collection

Almost any organic material works: agricultural waste (rice husks, corn cobs, wood chips), forestry residues, sewage sludge, algae, or dedicated energy crops. The type of feedstock influences the final biochar's properties.

Step 02

Drying

Feedstock is dried to reduce moisture content, ideally below 15–20%. High moisture reduces energy efficiency and biochar quality. Drying can be done with waste heat from the pyrolysis process itself, creating a closed-loop system.

Step 03

Pyrolysis

The dried material is heated to 300–700°C in an oxygen-limited environment. Slow pyrolysis (hours at lower temperatures) maximises biochar yield. Fast pyrolysis produces more bio-oil for fuel. The process co-produces syngas, which can generate electricity.

Step 04

Cooling & Quenching

The hot char must be carefully cooled and quenched to prevent spontaneous combustion. Water or liquid nutrients (compost tea, liquid fertiliser) are used for quenching — simultaneously cooling the char and beginning the activation process.

Step 05

Activation & Blending

Raw biochar is highly alkaline and initially nutrient-poor. "Charging" it with compost, manure, or urine fills its pores with beneficial microbes and nutrients — making it immediately plant-available when applied to soil.

Step 06

Soil Application

Biochar is typically applied at rates of 1–5 tonnes per hectare, incorporated into the top soil layer. Once in the ground, it functions indefinitely — ancient terra preta soils remain fertile after millennia.

Use Cases

Where biochar is
changing outcomes

Agriculture

Crop Production

Improves yield in acidic, degraded, and nutrient-poor soils — especially impactful for smallholder farming in sub-Saharan Africa and Southeast Asia.

Horticulture

Potting Mixes

Blended into growing media for nurseries and urban gardens, biochar improves aeration, prevents root rot, and reduces watering frequency.

Water

Filtration Systems

Used in constructed wetlands, bioswales, and point-of-use filters to remove nitrates, phosphorus, heavy metals, and pathogens.

Livestock

Animal Feed

Added to feed, biochar reduces gut pathogens, improves digestion, decreases enteric methane emissions, and enriches manure for composting.

Construction

Carbon-Negative Concrete

Biochar added to concrete mixes sequesters carbon in the built environment while improving insulation properties and reducing cement needed.

Remediation

Contaminated Land

Immobilises heavy metals and organic pollutants in contaminated industrial sites, brownfields, and mine tailings — making land suitable for reuse.

Energy

Co-production

Modern pyrolysis units generate biochar, bio-oil, and syngas simultaneously — creating energy-positive systems where heat powers the process.

Compost

Composting Aid

Accelerates composting by providing microbial habitat, absorbing odours and leachate, and improving the finished compost's nutrient profile.

Climate Action

A tool for the
carbon century

"Biochar represents one of the few truly carbon-negative technologies available to us today."

— IPCC Working Group III, Chapter on Carbon Dioxide Removal

The IPCC has identified biochar as a high-potential carbon dioxide removal (CDR) pathway with co-benefits for food security and land restoration — a rare win-win-win in climate solutions.

Unlike direct air capture, biochar uses biological carbon already cycled through crops and forests. It doesn't require new land, exotic minerals, or vast energy inputs — just existing agricultural waste streams transformed by heat.

Modelling by the Potsdam Institute and others suggests biochar could sustainably remove 1–2.6 gigatonnes of CO₂ per year by mid-century — equivalent to shutting down hundreds of coal power plants.

Critically, biochar's co-benefits mean it pays for itself in improved agricultural productivity long before carbon credits are factored in. This makes it accessible to farmers in the Global South, where it's needed most.

Carbon Math

Turning 1 tonne of dry woody biomass into biochar sequesters ~1.8 tonnes of CO₂ equivalent, while the pyrolysis process itself can generate ~0.5 MWh of usable heat energy.

Biochar shows the greatest benefits in degraded, sandy, or acidic soils with low organic matter. In already-fertile soils with high organic content, benefits are more modest. Soil testing before application ensures the right feedstock type and application rate for maximum impact.

Poorly made biochar can contain harmful PAHs and heavy metals from contaminated feedstocks. International standards (IBI, EBC) set quality thresholds for safe soil application. Certified biochar from reputable producers is safe and beneficial. Never apply large amounts of fresh, uncharged biochar — it can temporarily immobilise soil nitrogen.

Biochar currently costs $200–$800 per tonne depending on feedstock, scale, and region. At 1–5 t/ha application rates, this represents a significant upfront investment. However, carbon credit markets, energy co-production, and improved yields often make the full system economics positive. Costs are falling as scale increases.

Home gardeners can begin with small-scale retort kilns or cone kilns for backyard biochar production, or purchase certified biochar online.
