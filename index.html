01 — What is it

Carbon forged
by fire

Biochar is a stable, carbon-rich material produced by heating organic matter — wood, crop residue, manure — in a low-oxygen environment through a process called pyrolysis.


Unlike ordinary charcoal, biochar is purpose-made as a soil amendment or carbon sink. Its porous, sponge-like structure hosts billions of microorganisms and holds nutrients that would otherwise leach away, dramatically improving soil health over centuries.

02 — Benefits

What it does
for the earth

🌱

Soil Fertility

Biochar's porous structure retains water and nutrients like nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium — reducing fertiliser runoff and boosting crop yields by 10–40% in depleted soils.

🌡️

Carbon Sequestration

Converting biomass to biochar locks up carbon that would otherwise return to the atmosphere. One tonne of biochar contains roughly 3 tonnes of CO₂ equivalent, stable for hundreds of years.

💧

Water Retention

A single gram of biochar holds up to 6× its weight in water. In drought-prone regions this means less irrigation, fewer crop failures, and healthier groundwater recharge.

🦠

Microbial Habitat

The labyrinthine pore network provides shelter for beneficial fungi and bacteria, building the living web that drives nutrient cycling, disease suppression, and resilient ecosystems.

☠️

Contaminant Removal

Activated biochar adsorbs heavy metals, pesticides, and other pollutants from contaminated soils and water — a low-cost tool for remediation in the Global South.

🔋

Energy Co-product

The pyrolysis process generates syngas and bio-oil alongside biochar — turning waste biomass into a source of clean heat or electricity with net-negative emissions.

03 — How it's made

From biomass
to black gold

Pyrolysis transforms organic waste into one of nature's most durable materials in a matter of hours. The process is scalable — from a backyard kiln to an industrial reactor.

Source Biomass

Wood chips, rice husks, corn stover, sewage sludge, seaweed — almost any organic waste works. Drier feedstocks yield more char.

Dry & Prep

Moisture is reduced to below 20%. High moisture content wastes energy and reduces char quality; pre-drying is often the rate-limiting step.

Pyrolysis

Heated to 300–700 °C with minimal oxygen in a kiln or reactor. Temperature and residence time control porosity, surface area, and nutrient content.

Cooling & QC

Char is quenched and tested for pH, surface area (BET), and heavy metals. Quality standards (e.g. IBI, EBC) ensure safe, effective material.

Activate & Apply

Often inoculated with compost, microbes, or liquid fertiliser before soil application. Activation prevents the char from initially drawing nutrients from soil.

04 — Climate Role

A scalable
carbon drawdown

Unlike tree planting — where carbon can return via wildfire or decay — biochar represents a near-permanent carbon store. Scientists estimate biochar could draw down 1–2 billion tonnes of CO₂ annually by 2050 if deployed at scale.


It is one of few negative-emission technologies that also delivers immediate agricultural co-benefits, making it economically viable without requiring carbon markets to function first.

"Biochar is one of the few win-win-win solutions we have — it improves soils, sequesters carbon, and can be produced from waste streams."
— Johannes Lehmann, Cornell University

Key Research Areas

Terra Preta

Ancient Amazonian dark earths — the original biochar soils, still extraordinarily fertile 2,000 years on.

Permanence

Recalcitrance modelling shows 90% of biochar carbon persists beyond 500 years under typical soil conditions.

N₂O Suppression

Meta-analyses show biochar reduces soil nitrous oxide emissions by ~30%, a potent but under-reported climate benefit.

Carbon Credits

Biochar MRV methodologies are maturing; credits now fetch $100–200/t CO₂ on voluntary carbon markets.
